        How I Ended Up Living in a Van

    </h1>

    <p class="post-date">

        March 12, 2025

    </p>


    <!-- Replace image -->

    <img

    <article class="essay">


        <p>

            There wasn't a single dramatic moment when I decided to move into a van.

            Instead, it happened slowly.

        </p>


        <p>

            One road trip turned into another. Surfboards stayed in the car longer.

            Campsites became familiar. The feeling of returning home slowly shifted

            from a physical place to a collection of routines carried with me.

        </p>


        <p>

            Mornings began with checking the wind forecast and making coffee near

            the ocean. Some days felt productive. Others were spent reading,

            sketching, or watching changing light across the water.

        </p>


        <p>

            Living on the road taught me that a meaningful life isn't always built

            through accumulation. Sometimes it grows through subtraction.

        </p>


        <p>

            With fewer possessions, fewer plans, and fewer expectations,

            I found more room for attention.

        </p>


        <p>

            I started noticing details again:

            the smell of eucalyptus after rain,

            the shape of driftwood on empty beaches,

            and the quiet satisfaction of cooking dinner

            under an open sky.

        </p>


        <p>

            The road didn't solve every problem.

            It simply gave me enough space to listen

            to questions I had been avoiding.

        </p>


        <p>

            Looking back, living in a van was never really about travel.

            It was about learning a different relationship with time,

            place, and myself.

        </p>


    </article>
